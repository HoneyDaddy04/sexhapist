# Reigniting it.

I want to write to the woman who has been in the same relationship for a long time. Long enough that you used to know each other in a way you do not now. Long enough that the stretch of time without much sex has become its own background hum. You are not on the way out of the marriage. You love him. He loves you. But the thing that used to feel alive between you has gone elsewhere, and you do not know if it knows how to come back.

I have watched a lot of couples come back from this. Not all of them. The ones who do tend to share certain things. I want to give you those things now.

**Reigniting starts before the bedroom. Often months before.**
Couples who try to fix sexual disconnection by going straight to sex almost always fail. The body knows. It does not respond to scheduled fixing. What works is rebuilding the conditions in which desire was once possible: lightness, curiosity, time alone together that is not transactional, eye contact that lingers. The bedroom is the last room you renovate, not the first.

**One person almost always has to lead. It is not unfair.**
Many women wait for it to be mutual. We agreed on this together, we go in together, we both initiate. In long disconnection, that often does not happen, because both people are scared and scared people don't initiate. Usually one partner has to be willing to be slightly braver than the other for a while. To reach first. To name it first. To hold the warmth open even when it isn't being matched yet. That is not weakness. That is the work.

**Old fires do not relight as the same fire.**
This is the part most couples don't expect. The sex that comes back after long disconnection is rarely identical to the sex you had at thirty. It is slower, often more honest, often more connected, sometimes less athletic. Couples who try to recreate what they had at the beginning frequently get discouraged because the imitation never works. Couples who let themselves build something new on top of what they have learned about each other almost always end up with something better.

I had a woman once who told me, in our last session, that the sex she and her husband were having at fifty-two was the best of their marriage. She had been sure, at forty-six, that they were over. The thing they rebuilt was not the thing they used to have. It was something they had grown into.

If you and your partner have been disconnected for longer than you would like to admit, and you are not sure if it can come back, that is the question this work answers. Sometimes the answer is yes. Often, it is yes, in a different shape than you expected. And the way back almost always begins with one person willing to make the first reach.
