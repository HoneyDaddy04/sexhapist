# How to bring sex up.

Here is a moment a lot of women I work with describe almost word for word. They have been thinking about a conversation for weeks. Maybe months. They finally decide tonight is the night. They wait until the kids are asleep. They turn off the television. They take a breath, and they say something carefully phrased about wanting more sex, or different sex, or any sex, and within ninety seconds his face has closed and the conversation is over. He says he's sorry. He says he's tired. He says he doesn't know what's wrong with him. He goes upstairs.

She sits in the living room and replays what she said.

She did not say anything wrong. She brought up a real, important topic in good faith. So why did it land like a hand grenade?

It usually comes down to three things.

**The when matters more than the what.**
Most attempts to talk about sex happen too close to actual sex. In the bedroom. After a missed initiation. Right before sleep. These are all moments when his nervous system is already on guard. Even a gentle word about the topic will be heard as an evaluation of him at the place he feels most exposed. The conversations that go best almost always happen in neutral territory, far from the bed. On a walk. In the car. At brunch. Hours from anything sexual, when his guard is not up.

**Frame as longing, not lack.**
"I miss us" lands differently than "we never have sex." They are technically about the same thing, but his brain processes them in opposite directions. Lack is a problem he has caused. Longing is a feeling that he is part of. Most men want very badly to be the answer to your longing. Very few men want to be the cause of your problem. Frame matters.

**Open with a question, not a complaint.**
The fastest way to keep a man in the conversation is to ask him something instead of telling him something. "Have you noticed how busy we've been?" "Do you ever feel like we've drifted?" "What was it like for you when [specific time]?" Open with curiosity about him, not announcement of you. Once he is in the conversation as a participant, he has somewhere to go. Once he is in it as a defendant, he has nowhere to go but out.

I gave a client three things to try once. Walk, not bedroom. Longing, not lack. Question, not complaint. She picked a Saturday afternoon. They were walking by the river. She said, "Can I ask you something I've been thinking about? Have you been feeling close to me lately?" That was it. The whole opener.

He stopped walking. He told her, slowly, that he had not been feeling close. He had not known how to bring it up. They talked for over an hour about things that had nothing and everything to do with sex. The conversation that had been going badly for months started going well that day.

If you have a conversation in you that you have been afraid to start, this is where we figure out how to start it.
