# Inviting him in.

There is a particular flavor of nervousness women carry about asking for something specific in the bedroom. Not just more sex. Something specific. A pace. A word. A scene. A position they read about. A fantasy they have had since they were twenty-two. They want to bring it up, and they don't, because the imagined cost of bringing it up feels enormous. He might think she is broken. He might think she has been doing it with someone else. He might think she has been fantasizing about that all these years and he has been failing the test.

So she keeps the request in. The fantasy stays a private thing. The marriage stays the same. Both partners are slightly less present than they could be, and neither of them knows why.

I have had hundreds of versions of this conversation, and I want to give you what I have learned.

**Most men are relieved, not threatened, by a specific request.**
The story women tell themselves is that asking for something will hurt him. The story men actually live is the opposite. A man who is given a specific request gets a piece of information he has been missing his whole sexual life. He gets to know what to do. He gets to be useful. He gets to participate in your pleasure as a partner instead of guessing his way through it. The fear that "he'll feel inadequate" is real and almost always smaller than the relief he feels when you finally tell him what you want.

**Frame it as discovery, not correction.**
"I've been wanting to try something" lands differently than "we never do this and I want to." One opens a door. The other walks past him to do it. Most invitations work best when they sound like a thing the two of you are exploring together, not a deficit you are correcting. He wants to be invited into the next chapter of your sex life. He doesn't want to be informed he has been failing the previous one.

**Pace the asks. You don't need to give him the whole list.**
A common mistake is treating the conversation as a one-time event where you list every fantasy and request you have ever had. That is overwhelming for most men. What works better is small, occasional, specific. One thing. Try it. See how it goes. Notice what the request taught you about each other. Come back another night with another thing. The bedroom expands gradually, not in one evening.

I had a client who had been wanting, for ten years of marriage, to try something very specific that mattered to her a lot. She was in tears when she told me, because she was certain her husband would refuse. We worked out the script for weeks. She asked him on a Tuesday night, simply and without apology. He said, "I had no idea you wanted that. I'd love to."

That was it. Ten years of carrying it. One real sentence. A new chapter.

If you have a request in you that has been waiting, this is where we get it out of the dark.
