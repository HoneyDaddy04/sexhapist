# He shut down. In bed.

She came to me in March, and the way she described it, it sounded like a slow door closing. There was no fight. There was no other woman, as far as she knew. He just stopped reaching for her. Not all at once. The good night kiss got shorter. The hand on the small of her back disappeared. By the time she counted, it had been a year and three months since he had initiated anything in bed.

"I think he's gone off me," she said. "I don't know what I did."

Most of the women I see who carry this story arrive convinced of one of two things. Either they've become unattractive, or he's checked out. Both feel terrible. Both miss the point.

Here is what I have learned, watching this play out across hundreds of marriages.

**Most men do not stop wanting their partner. They stop trusting their own body to perform.**
Somewhere along the way (often after one bad night that they've never spoken about), a man begins to associate sex with potential humiliation. The answer his nervous system reaches for is to avoid the situation entirely. He doesn't tell you because he can't tell himself. He just stops initiating, and then he hopes you don't notice, and then he hopes you don't ask.

**The shutdown is rarely about you. It is almost always about him.**
Erectile worry, performance anxiety, a body that doesn't move the way it did at twenty-five, shame about weight or age, a stressful season at work, mild depression, a medication side effect nobody bothered to warn him about. Any one of these can quietly take a man's desire offline. Most men can't bring it up because they've spent their entire lives being told their job is to want it more than you do.

**He is not waiting for you to seduce him out of it.**
This is the cruel part. Many women, when they finally name what's happening, try to fix it by being sexier. Lingerie. Initiation. Compliments. It usually doesn't work, because the problem isn't desire. It's safety. He is in a small private war with himself, and what he needs first is permission to lose it without losing you.

There was a moment in the work with the woman in March. She had been describing what she'd tried (the lingerie, the late-night moves, the careful escalations), and I asked her what would happen if she said nothing about sex for a month. Just held him. Just made the bedroom a place where his body did not have to perform. She looked at me like I'd told her to give up.

But she tried it.

A few weeks in, on a Sunday afternoon, he started crying in the kitchen. He told her something he had been carrying since their second year of marriage, about an episode of bad sex he'd convinced himself she was still angry about. She wasn't. She'd forgotten it. He had been quietly bleeding for nine years.

That conversation didn't fix their sex life. But it started it. The reaching came back slowly, in small ways, the way it usually does. A hand on the back. A kiss that lingered. The first night he initiated, six weeks later, and was scared, and tried anyway, and she just held his hand.

If your husband or partner has gone quiet in bed and you don't know why, you are not the problem. He is not broken either. There is something underneath this that neither of you have had the language for, and most of the time it can be reached.

That is the work we do here.
