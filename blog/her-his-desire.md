# His desire dropped.

When a man's desire falls off, the woman in his life is usually the first to notice and the last to be told. There is a long, quiet period where she is trying to reason her way through it. Maybe he's tired. Maybe it's the new job. Maybe it's me. She does not bring it up because she does not want to make it bigger than it is. She thinks if she just waits, it will pass.

Sometimes it does. More often, it doesn't, and the silence hardens into a feature of the relationship.

I want to walk you through what is usually actually happening, because it almost always isn't what you fear.

**Male libido is more sensitive than the cultural script admits.**
The script says men want it always, more than us, regardless. Real life is more complicated. Male desire dips and surges with stress, sleep, hormones, mood, alcohol use, weight, certain medications, a new baby, an aging parent, a death, a season of overwork, an unresolved fight that lives in the body. Most men experience desire dips and don't have a vocabulary for them. So they don't say. They just go quiet.

**A dropped libido is a symptom. The cause is usually not in the bedroom.**
By the time desire has fallen off, the cause is almost always somewhere else in his life. Burnout. Depression he hasn't named. A health issue he's been ignoring. A medication side effect. A buildup of unspoken resentment. A specific moment from earlier in the relationship he never recovered from. The bedroom is where the symptom shows up. Treating the bedroom directly almost never works, because the bedroom is not where the problem lives.

**You are not the doctor. You are the witness.**
A common mistake is to take the dropped desire on as a project you must fix. Lingerie. Initiation. Books. Weekends. Sometimes those help. Mostly what helps is being the person who notices, says something kind, and gives him room to investigate his own life. "I've noticed you've felt far away lately. Not just from me, but from yourself. Is something going on?" That sentence opens more than any seductive gesture I've seen.

I had a couple where his desire had been low for three years. She had tried everything she knew. She came in convinced he had stopped loving her. He came in convinced he was broken. We worked together for two months before they realized he was clinically depressed and had been hiding it from himself since their second child was born. He started treatment. The desire came back, slowly, in the order desire usually returns. First the affection. Then the curiosity. Then the body.

If his desire has dropped and you do not know what to do, the answer is rarely on the surface. It is rarely about you. And it is almost always reachable, if both of you are willing to look.
