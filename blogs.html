For him · Desire

Desire. And where it went.

There's a man I used to see on Wednesdays. He came in twenty minutes early every week, sat in the green chair, and for the first six months he never once said the word "sex."

There's a man I used to see on Wednesdays. He came in twenty minutes early every week, sat in the green chair, and for the first six months he never once said the word "sex." We talked about his job. We talked about his father. We talked about the ache in his lower back that came and went on no schedule. And then one Wednesday in April he said, very quietly, "I think I'm broken."

He wasn't broken. He was thirty-seven, married eight years, two kids, and the desire he used to recognize in himself had gone somewhere he could not follow. It had not been replaced by something dramatic. It had just thinned out. The reach was still there. The wanting was not.

If you are reading this, you probably know what he meant.

We tend to talk about male desire as if it's a switch. On or off. Working or broken. A lot of the men I've worked with come in convinced something is wrong with them, because the simple version of the story (men want sex always, all the time, more than their partner) does not match what they actually feel. So they stay quiet. They blame work, sleep, age, the kids, the wife, themselves. They Google in incognito. They schedule a doctor's appointment they never go to.

Here is what I have learned, sitting across from hundreds of versions of that man.

Desire is a feedback loop, not a fuel tank.

You don't run out. You stop topping it up. Every hour spent in a state of low-grade anxiety, performance pressure, unresolved resentment, or pure exhaustion sends the system a message: now is not the time. Do that for long enough and the system stops volunteering. It waits to be told it's safe.

The thing that killed it is rarely the thing that brings it back.

Men come in wanting to fix their testosterone. Or their wife. Or their stamina. Sometimes those matter. More often, the thing in the way is something quieter. A moment from a year ago where you asked for something and the answer hurt. A version of yourself you stopped recognizing in the mirror. A creeping sense of being needed but not wanted.

You can rebuild it. It just isn't loud.

Real desire returns the way a stream comes back after a drought. Slowly, in the corners. A song you used to like. A glance across the kitchen. The first morning your body wakes up and you don't immediately reach for your phone. You do not muscle it back. You make conditions. You wait.

The man in the green chair came back six months later, slower in his shoulders, easier in his face, and told me his wife had asked him on a Tuesday night if he wanted to take a walk. He said yes. They walked for forty minutes and didn't talk about anything important. When they got home, he kissed her in the doorway. Nothing else happened that night.

But something had returned, and they both knew it.

If your desire feels like it has gone somewhere you can't reach, you are not broken. You are not finished. You are in the middle of a conversation your body is trying to have with you, and the first job is just to listen.

That is what we are doing here.

For him · Performance

Performance.

The first time it happened to David, he was thirty-one and wildly in love. He told me about it three years later, with the kind of careful slowness men use when they are circling something they have never said out loud.

The first time it happened to David, he was thirty-one and wildly in love. He told me about it three years later, with the kind of careful slowness men use when they are circling something they have never said out loud. They had been seeing each other six months. She was on top of him. She paused, in the most ordinary way, to take off a necklace that was getting in the way, and in that pause something in his head turned around and walked out.

He couldn't get it back that night. He couldn't get it back for two weeks. By the time he could, the relationship had already absorbed the silence, and they ended things the following spring without ever having a real conversation about it.

He came in to talk about something else. The performance thing came out by accident.

I have heard variations of David's story from hundreds of men. The pause before she put the necklace down. The first time after the baby. The night his boss called as he was getting into bed. The morning after a bad sleep. One thing pulls his attention out of his body and the body, deprived of attention, simply stops cooperating. The next time, his body is not the only thing in the room. His head is also there, watching, waiting to fail.

Here is what I want every man who has lived this to hear.

Performance anxiety is a focus problem, not a virility problem.

Erections do not happen because you are man enough. They happen because your nervous system has decided the situation is safe and your attention is in the right place. Yank attention away (by self-monitoring, by worrying, by trying) and the system reads it as a threat. The harder you push, the further it retreats. This is not a moral failing. It is wiring.

The cure is not more pressure. It is more permission.

Most men try to fix performance anxiety by gritting their teeth. They try to power through. They psych themselves up. None of it works, because the problem is exactly the part of you that is gritting and powering and psyching. The first move is to take sex off the line. Not forever. For long enough that your body stops bracing.

You will need a partner who knows what's happening.

This is the part most men try to avoid, and it is the part that breaks the pattern. The shame says: don't tell her, fix it first. The shame is wrong. A partner who knows you are working through this is a partner who will stop scanning your face for signs of fading interest. The script can be as simple as, "Sometimes my body checks out and it has nothing to do with you. I'm working on it." That sentence has rebuilt more sex lives than any pill.

David is married now. Different woman. He told me last summer that the anxiety still visits, occasionally, like an old roommate stopping by. But it does not own the room anymore.

If your head walked out of the bedroom and you don't know how to call it back, that is the work we do here.

For him · Connection

Connection.

Tunde had been married for eleven years when he came in and told me, in a tone I have heard so many times, "We're fine. We just live in different rooms."

Tunde had been married for eleven years when he came in and told me, in a tone I have heard so many times, "We're fine. We just live in different rooms."

He didn't mean literally. They slept in the same bed. They raised two children together. They took a holiday to Cape Coast every March. But somewhere along the way, the easy current that used to run between them had gone underground. They were partners. They were not lovers. They were both unhappy and neither of them knew how to bring it up.

This is the most common form of long-term-relationship suffering I see. Not affair. Not abuse. Not a fight that lit the house on fire. Just a slow, quiet drift. Two people who used to face each other and now mostly face the world together, side by side, never looking back at each other for very long.

Here is the thing about that drift.

Distance accumulates in micro-moments. So does closeness.

A relationship is not held together by anniversaries. It is held together by a thousand tiny exchanges that each take less than ten seconds. The look across the room when she laughs at something on TV. The hand on the small of the back walking through a crowd. The stupid, specific question that says: I am still curious about you. Stop doing those small things and the whole thing dries out, even when nothing is officially wrong.

The way back is shorter than you think.

Couples in long drift usually expect the climb back to be enormous. A trip. A reset. A conversation about everything. It almost never works that way. What works is one small shift, repeated. Ask one real question at dinner. Hold one hug for a few seconds longer. Look at her, properly, when she is talking. Most distance unravels not in dramatic gestures but in the willingness to be slightly more present than yesterday.

Connection is not a feeling first. It is a practice first.

You will not always feel close before you act close. You act close, and the feeling follows. Many men wait for desire or affection to return before they reach for their partner. That is the wrong order. Reach first, with no agenda, and the warmth has somewhere to come back to.

I told Tunde to put a sticky note on his bathroom mirror with one word: notice. For two weeks his job was to notice his wife once a day. Anything. Her hair. Something she said. The way she dealt with their son's tantrum. He was not allowed to compliment her. He was just allowed to notice.

Three weeks in, she asked him what was different. He said nothing. She said, "You're looking at me again."

If you and your partner have become roommates with shared paperwork, this is the room where we begin to climb out.

For him · Honesty

Honesty.

A man named Ola, forty-four, sat in my office for the better part of an hour describing a sexual dissatisfaction he had been carrying for almost a decade. Then, on the way out the door, almost as a joke, he turned around and said the thing he came in to say.

A man named Ola, forty-four, sat in my office for the better part of an hour describing a sexual dissatisfaction he had been carrying for almost a decade. He used very careful language. He said his wife was "amazing." He said their sex life was "fine." He said he was "grateful." Then, on the way out the door, almost as a joke, he turned around and said, "I just wish she'd ever, in our entire marriage, asked me what I actually liked."

There it was. The thing he came in to say. The thing he had not yet said to her, in nine years of marriage.

Most men I see have one of these. A request, a preference, a thing they used to do with a previous partner and miss, a fantasy, a longing. It sits in a small dark drawer that gets harder to open the longer it stays shut. They tell themselves it is selfish. They tell themselves it would hurt her feelings. They tell themselves she should already know.

Here is the trap.

Unspoken requests do not stay neutral. They sour.

What starts as "I'll just wait for the right moment" turns into "she should be able to read me" turns into "she doesn't really care about my pleasure" turns into resentment, and resentment is the slow death of attraction. The longer a man holds something back, the bigger and more loaded it gets, until even saying it out loud feels like an indictment.

Most partners want to know. They are afraid you don't.

Almost every woman I have ever spoken to wishes her man would tell her what he wanted. Not because she is desperate to perform. Because she is tired of guessing. The fear that "she'll be hurt if I ask for something different" is real but usually backwards. What hurts is being kept on the outside of his actual desire while pretending everything is fine.

You don't have to make it a confession. You can make it a curiosity.

The first time you say a thing out loud, it does not have to be a heavy speech. "I've been thinking about something I'd love to try" is a sentence. "I love this thing you do, and I want more of it" is a sentence. "Have you ever wondered what it would feel like if we..." is a sentence. None of those are confessions. All of them are doors.

I asked Ola if I could send him home with one sentence to try. He said yes. The sentence was, "There's something I've been wanting to tell you for a long time, and I'm a little nervous to say it." That was it. He was not allowed to follow it with the actual content for at least a week.

When he came back, he was a different shape. They had stayed up until two in the morning the night he said the sentence, and the actual content turned out to be much smaller than the weight of holding it.

If you have a sentence stuck in your throat that has been there for years, this is where we start to free it.

For him · Stamina

Stamina.

Lasting longer is a learnable skill. It is not a gift you were born with or denied. It is not a sign of how much you love your partner. It is closer to a sport.

I am going to start here with something most men have never been told plainly. Lasting longer is a learnable skill. It is not a gift you were born with or denied. It is not a sign of how much you love your partner. It is not a measure of how much of a man you are. It is closer to a sport. People learn it, train at it, get better at it.

The reason this surprises so many men is that almost no one teaches it. We teach driving. We teach cooking. We don't teach this. So men either trip over the right answers by accident, or they don't, and they spend years carrying private shame about a problem with a known solution.

Let me name the most common version of the problem first. A man finishes faster than he or his partner would like. Sometimes by a lot. Sometimes early in a relationship, sometimes after years. Sometimes always. Sometimes only with new partners. He has tried thinking about football, biting his cheek, taking himself out for a few seconds. None of it works the way he hoped. He starts avoiding sex.

Here is what I would tell him in my office.

Speed is mostly arousal management, not willpower.

You cannot grit your way out of finishing fast. The whole game is staying in the part of arousal that is intense but sustainable, and stepping back from the edge before you cross it. That requires noticing the edge before you arrive. Most men finish fast because they have never been taught to feel the gradient. They feel "fine, fine, fine, gone."

You build the skill alone before you bring it to a partner.

This is the unsexy part. Men who want to last longer almost always need to practice solo first. Slow down. Stop. Notice the sensation just before the point of no return, and then back off. Get familiar with the feeling of the edge. Do this until you can stay in that range for as long as you want. Then bring it to your partner. People who skip the solo work get frustrated and quit.

Talk to your partner about what's happening.

Stamina improves much faster when your partner is on the team. A man who has to perform in silence is also performing under pressure, and pressure shortens the runway. A simple "I'm working on lasting longer, can we slow down when I tap your hip" turns sex from a test into a project you are doing together. The pressure drops. The runway extends.

I have watched men who came in convinced they had a permanent physical defect rebuild their stamina in eight to twelve weeks of consistent practice. I have also seen men who didn't bother and stayed exactly where they started.

If you have been carrying this quietly, you don't have to. The shame is louder than the problem.

For him · Repair

Repair.

There is a kind of couple who comes in after the worst is over. The fight has already happened. The thing has already been said. The trust has already cracked. The question they want me to answer is always the same.

There is a kind of couple who comes in after the worst is over. The fight has already happened. The thing has already been said. The trust has already cracked, sometimes from one act, sometimes from a thousand small ones. They are not fighting anymore. They are sitting in two chairs across from me with the look of people who have just survived a fire and are not yet sure what they have left.

The question they want me to answer is always the same. Can we come back from this?

The honest answer is: that depends almost entirely on what you both do in the next ninety days.

I want to be careful with this one, because repair is not a five-step program. It is a posture. It is a way of facing each other after someone has been hurt and the hurt is still in the room. But there are patterns. Couples who come back share a lot. Couples who don't, also share a lot.

Repair is not "moving on." It is moving through.

The temptation, especially for men, is to skip ahead. Get past it. Stop talking about it. Make her feel better by being normal again. That is not repair. That is suppression with a smile. Repair means staying in the conversation about what happened, on her timeline, for as long as it takes for the wound to actually start closing. If you rush it, you don't get her back. You get a quieter version of her.

Accountability without defense is the thing that turns the corner.

The single most powerful sentence in repair is the one without a "but" in it. "I did this. It hurt you. I see why." When a man can sit in that sentence without immediately explaining or defending or contextualizing, his partner's nervous system gets a piece of information she has been missing: he understands what he did. That single piece of information starts the clock on healing. Defending stops it.

Trust comes back through repetition, not declarations.

You do not earn trust back by promising. You earn it back by doing small, consistent things, over and over, for a longer time than feels fair. Coming home when you said you would. Telling her where you are. Not flinching when she brings it up again three months later. The small things, repeated, are the only currency that buys back what was lost.

I had a couple last year who almost ended their marriage in November and were laughing together at a wedding by the following August. The husband said something to me on the way out of our last session that I have repeated to other men ever since: "I stopped trying to convince her I had changed and started just behaving like I had."

If something has cracked between you and you don't know if you can come back from it, that is the question this work answers.

For him · Curiosity

Curiosity.

A man came in once, sat down, and the first thing out of his mouth, before he even said his name, was, "I have a question I've never been able to ask anyone."

A man came in once, sat down, and the first thing out of his mouth, before he even said his name, was, "I have a question I've never been able to ask anyone."

I waited.

He looked at the ceiling. He looked at the door. He looked at his hands. Eventually he asked the question, and it was, in clinical terms, a very small question. A question about an extremely common bodily function. He was forty-six and had been wondering about it since he was a teenager.

That is the thing about sexual questions men carry. They are almost never as exotic as the man fears. They are almost always things many other men also wonder about. But men have very few places to ask, because most of the channels available to them treat the questions either as a joke (locker room, comedy) or a problem (porn, doctor's office). There is a missing third place: somewhere quiet, where you can ask the small and the strange and the embarrassing without anyone making it bigger than it is.

Here is what I want every man holding an unasked question to know.

Most of what worries you is normal. The worry is the bigger problem.

By far, the most common outcome when a man finally asks the thing is that the thing is fine. Bodies do that. People want that. That changes over a lifetime. The pattern you are noticing is well-known. The questions that turn out to need actual attention are rare. The shame that has built up around the question is usually the larger thing to deal with, because it has been training you to hide for years.

Curiosity in your own sexuality is an asset, not a liability.

Men get the message early that wanting to know more about sex, their bodies, or their partner's body is somehow shameful. That message is wrong. The men with the best sex lives I see are the ones who stayed curious. They asked. They learned. They updated. The men with the most stuck sex lives are the ones who decided long ago that they should already know everything and stopped asking.

A safe place to ask is more important than the right answer.

What men need first is not information. It is permission. A space where you can say "I have wondered about this for years, and here's what I want to know," without bracing for judgment. Once that space exists, the questions come out faster than you expect, and most of them turn out to be much lighter than they felt while you were holding them.

The man with the small question, by the way, had three more questions after that one. We covered them all in the same hour. He left lighter than he came in.

If you have a question that has been sitting in you for a long time, this is where you can finally put it down.

For her · He shut down

He shut down. In bed.

She came to me in March, and the way she described it, it sounded like a slow door closing. There was no fight. There was no other woman, as far as she knew. He just stopped reaching for her.

She came to me in March, and the way she described it, it sounded like a slow door closing. There was no fight. There was no other woman, as far as she knew. He just stopped reaching for her. Not all at once. The good night kiss got shorter. The hand on the small of her back disappeared. By the time she counted, it had been a year and three months since he had initiated anything in bed.

"I think he's gone off me," she said. "I don't know what I did."

Most of the women I see who carry this story arrive convinced of one of two things. Either they've become unattractive, or he's checked out. Both feel terrible. Both miss the point.

Here is what I have learned, watching this play out across hundreds of marriages.

Most men do not stop wanting their partner. They stop trusting their own body to perform.

Somewhere along the way (often after one bad night that they've never spoken about), a man begins to associate sex with potential humiliation. The answer his nervous system reaches for is to avoid the situation entirely. He doesn't tell you because he can't tell himself. He just stops initiating, and then he hopes you don't notice, and then he hopes you don't ask.

The shutdown is rarely about you. It is almost always about him.

Erectile worry, performance anxiety, a body that doesn't move the way it did at twenty-five, shame about weight or age, a stressful season at work, mild depression, a medication side effect nobody bothered to warn him about. Any one of these can quietly take a man's desire offline. Most men can't bring it up because they've spent their entire lives being told their job is to want it more than you do.

He is not waiting for you to seduce him out of it.

This is the cruel part. Many women, when they finally name what's happening, try to fix it by being sexier. Lingerie. Initiation. Compliments. It usually doesn't work, because the problem isn't desire. It's safety. He is in a small private war with himself, and what he needs first is permission to lose it without losing you.

There was a moment in the work with the woman in March. She had been describing what she'd tried (the lingerie, the late-night moves, the careful escalations), and I asked her what would happen if she said nothing about sex for a month. Just held him. Just made the bedroom a place where his body did not have to perform. She looked at me like I'd told her to give up.

But she tried it.

A few weeks in, on a Sunday afternoon, he started crying in the kitchen. He told her something he had been carrying since their second year of marriage, about an episode of bad sex he'd convinced himself she was still angry about. She wasn't. She'd forgotten it. He had been quietly bleeding for nine years.

That conversation didn't fix their sex life. But it started it. The reaching came back slowly, in small ways, the way it usually does. A hand on the back. A kiss that lingered. The first night he initiated, six weeks later, and was scared, and tried anyway, and she just held his hand.

If your husband or partner has gone quiet in bed and you don't know why, you are not the problem. He is not broken either. There is something underneath this that neither of you have had the language for, and most of the time it can be reached.

That is the work we do here.

For her · The dry spell

The dry spell.

A friend of a friend called me a few years ago, late on a Tuesday, and asked me a question I have heard, in different forms, more times than I can count. "When did you and your husband last have sex?"

A friend of a friend called me a few years ago, late on a Tuesday, and asked me a question I have heard, in different forms, more times than I can count. "When did you and your husband last have sex?" That was the opening line. No greeting. No preamble. She skipped the whole pleasantry tier.

She had been keeping a private count. She was at four months. She had not told anyone. She was certain she was the only woman in her circle living this. She was, of course, not. She was simply the only woman in her circle who had decided to ask.

If you are reading this with your own number in the back of your head, hello. You have a lot of company.

The dry spell, in the kind of relationship where two people love each other and nothing terrible has happened, is one of the most common and least talked-about features of long-term partnership. It usually does not arrive with a thunderclap. It arrives by accumulation. Babies. Work. Aging parents. A bad few months that became a habit. A growing mismatch in energy. Then one day you look up and a season of your relationship has gone by without much physical contact and you are not sure exactly when that started.

Here is what I want women who are in one of these to know.

A dry spell is information, not a verdict.

It does not mean he doesn't love you. It does not mean you are no longer attractive. It does not mean the relationship is broken. It usually means something underneath has been ignored for too long and the body, which is honest, has gone quiet. The work is to figure out what that something is. The work is not to panic.

The longer it goes, the less it is about sex.

After a while, the not-having-sex becomes its own atmosphere in the relationship. Both people are aware. Both people are walking around the topic. Bringing it up feels increasingly loaded. This is the part where many couples freeze. They wait for the other person to break the silence, and the silence keeps not breaking, and time keeps passing. Naming it gently, without blame, is almost always the first move out.

You don't have to fix it. You have to ask about it.

The pressure women often put on themselves in a dry spell is to be the one who solves it. Be sexier. Initiate more. Plan a weekend away. Sometimes those help. More often what helps is a conversation that doesn't try to schedule sex and instead tries to understand what has changed. "I miss this. I've been trying not to make it a problem. Can we talk about what's been happening for both of us?" is a sentence that has restarted a lot of bedrooms.

The friend of a friend stayed on the phone with me for over an hour. By the end of it she had a plan. Not for sex. For a conversation. Two weeks later she texted me a single line. "We talked. There's a lot to work with." A few months after that, the count had started again, in a happier direction.

If you are in your own dry spell and don't know who is allowed to break the silence, this is where we begin.

For her · What he won't say

What he won't say.

Women who come to me about their husbands almost always arrive with the same baseline assumption: he is fine. The possibility that he might be carrying something he hasn't told her gets very little airtime.

Women who come to me about their husbands almost always arrive with the same baseline assumption: he is fine. Or, more precisely, that whatever is happening between them sexually is a function of how she is showing up. If something has gone quiet, she did something. If something is missing, she is missing it. The possibility that he might be carrying something he hasn't told her gets very little airtime, because nothing about how men move through the world tends to advertise that he is carrying it.

Let me tell you what I see, hour after hour, in the privacy of the room.

A lot of men, especially after thirty-five, are walking around with sexual worries they have never said to anyone. Not their friends. Not their doctor. Not their wife. Sometimes not even fully to themselves. The worry sits in a small private compartment that slowly leaks into the rest of the marriage in ways the wife feels but cannot name. He is more distant. He is more avoidant. He picks fights at bedtime. He stops initiating. She wonders what she did. He does not have the language for what he has done.

Here is some of what is usually in that compartment.

Performance worry that pre-dates the relationship.

Many men have had one or two experiences in their lives where their body did not do what they wanted it to. They never spoke about it. They built up a low-grade dread around being expected to perform. Years later, that dread is still active. It is the thing you feel when he gets quiet around the bedroom. It often has nothing to do with you and everything to do with a moment in 2007.

Body shame they would never tell you about.

Men are taught to act like they don't care how they look. Many of them do, intensely, and the older they get, the more they care, and the less they will admit it. Weight. Hairline. Stomach. The way they look in the mirror has been shifting for years and they have been pretending not to notice. By the time their wife sees them, they are already braced. That is what is happening when he won't undress with the lights on.

A version of desire that he does not know how to bring to you.

Men have inner lives. Many of them have a version of what they would love their sex life to look like that they have never offered, because they assume she would think they were strange, or perverted, or that she would be hurt that the version isn't her exactly as she is. So he keeps it inside, lives it in private, and the actual marriage gets the watered-down public version of his desire. She feels something missing and she is right.

The reason any of this matters is that you cannot meet a man in a place he will not show you. A lot of marriages get stuck for years on the wrong question. She is asking, "What more do I need to do?" The actual question is, "What is he not telling me?"

I am not telling you to interrogate him. I am telling you to suspect there is more than he has shown. And to make a small, ongoing space in your relationship where the things he never thought he could say have somewhere to land.

That is what we are doing here.

For her · Bringing it up

How to bring sex up.

Here is a moment a lot of women describe almost word for word. They have been thinking about a conversation for weeks. They take a breath, they say something carefully phrased, and within ninety seconds his face has closed.

Here is a moment a lot of women I work with describe almost word for word. They have been thinking about a conversation for weeks. Maybe months. They finally decide tonight is the night. They wait until the kids are asleep. They turn off the television. They take a breath, and they say something carefully phrased about wanting more sex, or different sex, or any sex, and within ninety seconds his face has closed and the conversation is over. He says he's sorry. He says he's tired. He says he doesn't know what's wrong with him. He goes upstairs.

She sits in the living room and replays what she said.

She did not say anything wrong. She brought up a real, important topic in good faith. So why did it land like a hand grenade?

It usually comes down to three things.

The when matters more than the what.

Most attempts to talk about sex happen too close to actual sex. In the bedroom. After a missed initiation. Right before sleep. These are all moments when his nervous system is already on guard. Even a gentle word about the topic will be heard as an evaluation of him at the place he feels most exposed. The conversations that go best almost always happen in neutral territory, far from the bed. On a walk. In the car. At brunch. Hours from anything sexual, when his guard is not up.

Frame as longing, not lack.

"I miss us" lands differently than "we never have sex." They are technically about the same thing, but his brain processes them in opposite directions. Lack is a problem he has caused. Longing is a feeling that he is part of. Most men want very badly to be the answer to your longing. Very few men want to be the cause of your problem. Frame matters.

Open with a question, not a complaint.

The fastest way to keep a man in the conversation is to ask him something instead of telling him something. "Have you noticed how busy we've been?" "Do you ever feel like we've drifted?" "What was it like for you when [specific time]?" Open with curiosity about him, not announcement of you. Once he is in the conversation as a participant, he has somewhere to go. Once he is in it as a defendant, he has nowhere to go but out.

I gave a client three things to try once. Walk, not bedroom. Longing, not lack. Question, not complaint. She picked a Saturday afternoon. They were walking by the river. She said, "Can I ask you something I've been thinking about? Have you been feeling close to me lately?" That was it. The whole opener.

He stopped walking. He told her, slowly, that he had not been feeling close. He had not known how to bring it up. They talked for over an hour about things that had nothing and everything to do with sex. The conversation that had been going badly for months started going well that day.
